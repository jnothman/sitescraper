Frequent flyer float may be grounded

  • Font Size: Decrease Increase
  • Print Page: Print

Steve Creedy, Aviation writer | September 22, 2008

THE global financial crisis looks set to delay an ambtious $800 million Qantas plan to float up to 40 per cent of its frequent flyer scheme.

Management have spent the past year setting up the rewards program -- which has more than 5 million members and is estimated by analysts to be worth about $2 billion -- as a separately accountable unit and restructuring the scheme to make it more attractive to users and potential investors.

But incoming chief executive Alan Joyce raised doubts about the move during the handover of the airline's first Airbus A380 inToulouse, France, over the weekend.

Mr Joyce said the move had always been subject to market conditions and these were currently "very weak".

"I think in the current environment, it's a board decision. We'll make recommendations to the board and we'll be talking about it in the board meeting this month," Mr Joyce said.

"But we've always said we had to maximise value and if market conditions were weak we wouldn't do it, so that's probably as far as I'll go at the moment."

Despite the potential delay to the float and the ravages hitting financial markets, Qantas executives on an international investor roadshow said they were not having problems raising finance.

They also did not believe the crisis was having a significant impact on the airline's traffic figures and said they remained comfortable with current market guidance -- this year's pre-tax profit would be in the middle of analyst estimates.

Qantas chief executive Geoff Dixon said the carrier was able to finance its aircraft orders in a variety of ways and on "very competitive terms".

"We're not having any difficulty financing our aircraft," Mr Dixon said. "I'm assuming, because we keep getting asked the question, others are.

"But we've got a variety of mechanisms available to us and we're a very attractive airline, one of the financially strongest in the world, so we do not have any problems in the current environment," Mr Dixon added.

Qantas also does not seem to be sharing the pain of some other Southeast Asian carriers when it comes to falling demand for business travel.

Mr Joyce said the impact of the financial crisis was difficult to gauge because of "noise" from industrial action by engineers earlier this year.

He believed the spike in fuel prices had stopped some people from travelling, but this had started to reverse as the cost of fuel fell.

But he said demand in the past few weeks had been "very strong" and suggested one reason for this was that the Australian economy and a big part of the airline's travel was still driven by the resources boom.

"So that to us is very different than some of the carriers (that depend on financial services)," he said, citing poor August figures by Hong Kong-based Cathay Pacific.

Mr Joyce also admitted the airline's poor on-time performance, which he continued to blame on the engineers' industrial action, had also affected demand.

But he said reliability was now back in the 90 per cent range.

"I think we're sitting at around 80 per cent for the month and that's a lot better than the 60s we were getting in July," he said. "We think by the end of September it will be back to normal and it will be back in the mid-80s, where Qantas traditionally sits."

Mr Joyce said Qantas also had a number of different options if travel did start to slow, including retiring aircraft, cutting flights and giving people the option of trading down to a cheaper cabin, although there was no sign of this happening yet.

"And, of course, we have Jetstar and Qantas as vehicles (we can) utilise, depending on what happens with the market circumstances," he said. "So we think we have more options than most carriers to be to cope with that type of environment."

Qantas is looking to expand its order of A380s but says it will keep the order within its current planned capital expenditure on aircraft of $35 billion. It currently has 20 A380s on order with options for four more and purchase rights on a further 12.

The A380 was more than two years late and the airline is currently trying to find out if there will be further delays in the Boeing 787 that will form the other major plank of its fleet.

Both are extremely efficient and give airlines a competitive advantage in a high fuel-cost environment.

Mr Joyce said Boeing was at this stage sticking with a November 2009 delivery date, but it was due to provide an update on the impact a strike by machinists.
